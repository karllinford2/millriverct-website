Mill River Estuary Foundation

The waterway where the Mill River meets Southport Harbor belongs to no one and yet to everyone. Preserving it will require the sustained commitment of neighbors, civic organizations, public officials, and stewards of Fairfield’s heritage working together. The Mill River Estuary Foundation invites the support of all who value this special place — through charitable contributions, volunteer service, and the sharing of expertise — so that the area may continue to thrive as both a natural refuge, a recreational haven and a treasured link to our past.

↓

The Mission:

The Mill River Estuary Foundation is being formed exclusively for charitable and educational purposes within the meaning of Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986. Its purpose is to preserve, protect, restore, and beautify this unique stretch of waterway and its environs, from just Perry’s Mill Pond downstream through upper Southport Harbor for the benefit of the public and for future generations.

To advance this mission, the Foundation will raise and deploy funds to maintain, repair, and improve the waterway and its surroundings; to support the natural habitats and wildlife that depend on this estuary; to promote public education and appreciation of the area’s ecological and historical significance; and to partner with the Town of Fairfield, the State of Connecticut, Department of Energy and Environmental Protection, and other nonprofit and civic organizations that share these goals.

Our Focus:

The Water: Where Long Island Sound’s tides meet the Mill River’s fresh flow, a brackish estuary sustains rich plant, marine, and bird life. Keeping its water levels balanced is vital to that future — which is why MREF aims to maintain them by restoring the historic Tide Mill Dam.

Aquatic Life: Estuaries are often called the “nurseries of the sea,” because so many animals reproduce and spend the early part of their lives there. Salty seawater mixes with fresh water draining from the land to create habitats with incredibly nutrient-rich environment that are not found elsewhere. These sheltered shallows and underwater grasses provide the perfect safe haven and feeding ground for young fish and crabs to mature before venturing into the open ocean. It is the goal of MREF to establish a more accommodative anadromous fish migration and spawning passage (Blueback Herring, Alewives, American Eels, etc.) as well as support other estuarine species such as Blue Crabs (and other crustaceans), Diamondback Terrapins, and countless invertebrates and improve aquatic diversity and health in Mill River.

The Banks. Native estuary plants, like marsh vegetation and seagrasses, act as a sponge that traps and filters out sediments and toxic runoff from rivers and streams before the water reaches the ocean. However, there are abundant invasive species that need to be controlled and adding more native grasses along the banks of the river helps not only with its preservation but adds to the beauty for all of us. Furthermore, a goal of MREF is to explore how to reduce sedimentation in the Upper Southport Harbor to help that unique area thrive as an important breeding ground for oysters, salt water marine life and extensive recreational use.

Avian Life: With its unique and diverse ecosystem the area is home to several important species of bird, including Bald Eagles, Ospreys, Cormorants, Swans, Blue Herons and Egrets, along with countless waterfowl, ducks, geese and other taxa. With the improved health and diversity of the estuary, all avian life will directly benefit.

Our First Priority

The Foundation’s first priority is to support the repair and upgrades of the Tide Mill Dam near the eastern portion of the Harbor Road bridge. For more than three centuries, the Tide Mill Dam has regulated the flow of water from the Mill River into Southport Harbor. Over the last several years the dam has fallen into disrepair, which was only exacerbated by the ice floes from this challenging winter of 2025/2026. The result has troubling consequences: water levels north of the bridge have dropped, disturbed flows downstream have allowed debris and sediment to accumulate, and the navigable channels within the harbor have begun to shift. Restoring this historic structure is essential to the ecological health of the estuary, to the safe navigation of the harbor, and to the preservation of the natural and historical character of the Upper Harbor itself.

The Foundation recognizes that the repair of the Tide Mill Dam is a substantial undertaking that will require close coordination with, and the approvals of, multiple regulatory authorities — including the Connecticut Department of Energy and Environmental Protection (including its Dam Safety and Inland Water Resources programs), the United States Army Corps of Engineers, and the Town of Fairfield, among others. The Foundation is committed to working cooperatively with each of these authorities and with all other stakeholders to ensure that any repair work is undertaken responsibly, lawfully, and in a manner that protects both the resource and the public interest.

The Tide Mill & the Grist Mill Dam

Around 1722, a grist mill was raised in the middle of the Mill River, on what is now Harbor Road. Unlike the brook-fed water mills of the inland hills, this was a tide mill — it borrowed its power not from a falling stream but from the rise and fall of the sea itself.

A dam stretched across the tidal river, fitted with one-way gates. Each rising tide pushed seawater through the gates to fill a mill pond behind the dam. At high water the gates swung shut, trapping the pond. Then, as the tide ebbed, the miller opened a sluice and let the captured water rush back out — turning a great wheel that drove the millstones grinding the neighborhood’s corn and grain into meal and flour. The mill ground grain on this spot for well over two hundred years.

How a tide mill keeps time with the sea

  1. 1

    Flood tide fills the pond

    The incoming tide flows through the dam’s gates and quietly fills the mill pond.

  2. 2

    High tide locks the gates

    At the top of the tide the gates close on their own, holding a pond full of seawater.

  3. 3

    Ebb tide turns the wheel

    As the sea drops, the miller releases the pond through a sluice; the rushing water spins the wheel and the stones grind.

  4. 4

    The schedule shifts daily

    Because the tides arrive about 50 minutes later each day, the miller’s working hours slid around the clock — grain was ground twice a day, whenever the sea allowed.

A few interesting facts

  • 1637 The Pequot War’s “Great Swamp Fight” took place nearby; survivors took refuge among the Sasqua who lived along these waters.
  • 1639 English settlers founded the village at the river’s mouth and called it “Mill River.”
  • 1722 A tide-powered grist mill was built in the river on today’s Harbor Road.
  • 1830s “Mill River” became “Southport” as the harbor grew into a thriving shipping port.
  • ~1950s After more than two centuries, the old mill finally fell silent — the dam and tidal pond still mark the harbor today.
